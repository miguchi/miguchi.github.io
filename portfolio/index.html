Design Philosophy

As a former biochemist, I think of the design process as if it were the scientific method: research, hypothesize, experiment to collect data, and use data to prove or disprove your hypothesis.

Empathize

User research is my strength. Talking and listening to real people is invaluable in the product design process. I focus on asking questions around an informal product hypothesis, and then analyze the answers to determine if my hypothesis was on the right track. I began building this skill while working as a high school teacher in New York City; I became very aware that students do not tell you what they need. You must infer their needs from their responses.

Define

While I admit to having an informal hypothesis in the empathize stage, the define stage is where my hypothesis is developed. I synthesize user-research into personas and determine user-goals. I construct my hypothesis by defining the problem to be solved and measurable outcomes as a result of solving the problem.

Ideate

Once the team understands who the users are (with the help of personas), I believe in team ideation using whiteboards and sketches to determine solutions to the problem. If the feature is smaller and does not need to involve the entire team, I will ideate individually, narrow my ideas, and present them formally or informally to stakeholders and developers for feedback and further refinement.

Prototype and Test

I group prototyping and testing into one stage because I believe that prototyping and testing happens simultaneously. I will prototype with paper and pencil or high-function prototypes to validate ideas with users. Users must see our design hypotheses in order to prove or disprove our product ideas. Data-driven decisions is what I strive for.

Administration System

user research, information architecture, interaction design

Designed administration system for management of product licenses, students, classes, schools, and districts.

Administration System

Role

user experience designer, product manager

Responsibilities

end-to-end product design, including competitive analysis, paper prototypes, low-fidelity wireframes, annotated wireframes, high function prototypes, usability testing, sketches, user-flows, input field validation guidelines, user stories, backlog grooming

Problem

The teacher-facing side of our product, the administration system, was quickly aging-out. The main purpose of the admin system was to distribute the product to students. In addition, the administration system was built for one admin user-level, teachers, when in practice we needed to incorporate multiple admin user-levels. How might we update our administration system for administrators to manage students, staff, and products?

Requirements

  • Use existing navigation scheme put in place by previous developer
  • Scale—accommodate hundreds of thousands of users
  • Flexible UI that can work with different user-levels and fit each user’s task flows
  • Incorporate various administration levels, including district administators, school administrators, teachers, and customer service

Tools Used

Axure, InDesign, Illustrator, Sketch, pencil and paper

Solution

Two main interfaces, table views and forms, exist throughout the admin system that can incorporate four different user-levels and the actions required for each user.

Team

project manager, 4 developers, 2 QAs

Design Timeline

3 months

Process

When I was first given this project, I set out to answer several questions:

  1. What are the objects that are needed in the system, or in other words, what and who are we managing?
  2. Which actions need to be performed on objects by each user-level?
  3. What information does each user-level need about each object?

Object mapping

After gathering user requirements from marketing and completing a competitive analysis, I mapped the objects in the system to the actions needed and information that could be displayed. This helped me to make sense of what objects were needed in the system.

Paper Prototypes

At the beginning of the project, it was one developer and I working within a short timeframe to set up the foundation for the rest of the project. Because of this, we used a lean framework to develop the admin system. We determined the best way to display thousands of objects would be in table form, with the ability to select an object from the table and perform an action on the item. The actions would be available as buttons at the top of the table. We agreed that the only action that this pattern would not incorporate is editing the object. Editing would take place in a separate form. Clicking the object within the table would take the user to an editable form. Since editing would take place in a form, we were able to separate what we needed to display to the user with the amount of information that needed to be stored for each object. This would also allow for flexibility in the amount of columns we could display in the table.

Iteration 1 Sitemap

Using the tables and forms we had sketched and the object mapping exercise, I created an architecture that could be used for all user-levels. District users were defined as district coaches, district leaders, and instructional technology specialists and were given the highest level of permissions. School users incorporated principals, vice principals, department heads, and school IT. The teacher user was a teacher within a school and had the lowest level of permissions.

Iteration 1 Prototype and Usability Testing

The developer I was working with had been working in parallel on the tables, forms, and backend while I ironed out the information architecture. We were able to create a high function prototype to test on users to validate our assumptions thus far. Due to the research, we knew identified school IT personnel as our target users. Within schools, this role has the responsibility of setting up products for all of the teachers and students within a school. Users were given tasks such as:

  • You have created a class called “English Period 4.” Given a list of students, add students to the class.
  • It is the beginning of the school year and you are prepping for beginning of the year meetings. First up on the agenda is distributing the classes and students in those classes to teachers. Print off the materials to give each teacher the list of students in each class so that they can start preparing.
  • A parent calls in and explains that the information for her son, Harry Miller, is wrong. There is a typo in his last name. In addition, he can’t login to access his homework. When he tries to login to the system she gets a message saying “Incorrect username or password.” Attempt to help her resolve both issues.

Iteration 2 Sitemap

Several factors led to an iteration of the sitemap. Some of the highlights include:

  1. We narrowed our MVP down to focus on the school-user.
  2. Usability feedback led to a revision in navigation to tables within tables.
  3. The amount of actions available per table was trimmed to maintain our timeline.

Successes and Learnings

At the onset of the project, I faced pushback from stakeholders on reorganizing the information architecture of the admin system. There was hesitation to reorganize the existing 20+ pages that were deemed necessary to have a functioning admin system. I was able to align all stakeholders once they understood that the same amount of functions, and more, were possible within 12 pages of content using two main containers. For example, our former system allowed a user to reset a student password four pages deep into the userflow, while our new system allows users to reset a student’s password on the first page.


While I designed for the future functionality for district and teacher users, as was requested, the scope of the project was outside the timeframe my team faced. I learned my lesson in narrowing focus based on the size of my team. Throughout the project, I communicated with developers and testers to discuss the feasibility and details of each of the features within the admin system. Because of the team working together to trim and create the product, we were able to release on time.

Data Visualization

user research, information architecture, content strategy, interaction design

Designed solutions for educators to view student proficiency on assignments and state standards. This project is an extension of my prior work creating an organizational database of content.

Data Visualization

This project is an extension of my prior work creating a database.

Role

user experience designer, product manager

Responsibilities

user goals, competitive analysis, information architecture, annotated wireframes, usability testing, user stories, backlog grooming

Problem

Data-driven instruction is a hot topic in education. Educators need a way to quickly determine student progress in order to adjust their curriciulum to the needs of their students. How might we use the information within our database of problems to create reports for educators?

Requirements

  • Utilize the problem database structure to display relevant information
  • Ability to view student progress based on state standards
  • Have a maximum amount of data that can be displayed so that load time is fast
  • Incorporate work that had already been started on the front and backends for this project

Solution

Educators are slow to adopt technology and shy away from complex reports, so I created a UI that is simple and consistent between all reports. The flexibility with each report is rooted in the filters available on each page.

Team

project manager, 4 developers, 2 QAs

Design Timeline

6 weeks

Process

This project had already been started before I was handed over all design responsibilities. I was asked to step in because the design of the project was veering off track. A prior developer had starting designing 15 reports, and my task was to determine user goals and incorporate that with his work.

Competitive Analysis

When viewing what had already been completed, I noticed that the existing reports could be categorized in several ways: standards-based, assignments-based, and grading task reports. A standards-based report displayed how many standards had been assessed. An assignments-based report displayed the grades for each student for a particular assignment. A grading task report showed the number of assignments that the teacher needed to grade and the problems that needed to be graded.

I completed a competitive analysis of other reports in online education products. In my research, I came to the conclusion that we were unique in offering both standards- and assignments-based reports. Other companies only offered standards-based reports.

Through my research, I discovered that the mental model teachers have of grading tasks is a gradebook. Teachers did not expect to find grading tasks within the reports section of our product. I suggested removing the grading task reports and functions to a gradebook section of the product.

User Goals

Five different users would need access to our reports: district leaders, school leaders, teachers, students, and parents. Each user would need to be presented different variations of the same data, based on their user needs. I grouped parents and students together because we had no plans to develop a parent only interface and their needs were similar to students.

District needs

  • Are our programs producing student learning?
  • Which schools need more assistance based on overall performance or standards performance?

School needs

  • How are we doing overall?
  • Which teachers and students are excelling or need improvement?
  • Which standards need more professional development?

Teacher needs

  • How can I differentiate my class?
  • Which students need more help?
  • Which students need more challenging work?

Student/Parent needs

  • How am I doing?
  • Which lessons and standards do I need to work on?
  • Which lessons and standards am I excelling in?

Types of Reports

Based on the use-cases, I created high-level descriptions of the types of reports that each type of user might expect.

Information Architecture

To fit the use-cases, four pages were needed. Drilling down into information in the report would take the user to the next report.

Filters

Because the pages for each user needed to be similar for timeline purposes, filters were the key to customizing reports for each user-level. Filters would allow each of the pages to display the relevant information for each user: District, School, Teacher, or Student/Parent.

Successes and Learnings

One of the greatest successes of this project was that the database creation I had worked on previously could now be shown visually to educators. The database proved to be flexible enough to display the data in multiple formats. While I was designing the details for our database, I had always had the hopes that I would be part of designing the data visualization portion for educators.

This is the first end-to-end feature that I designed. From designing reports, I improved my communication skills between developers and stakeholders. I refined my deliverables and customized them to my audience to best communicate with the developers and persuade stakeholders to approve the plans. Because of this, I was able to align all stakeholders and developers to agree to a plan of action.

The most challenging aspect of this project was facing the realization of load times for the reports I was proposing. Many of my ideas had to be refined due to the amount of information (and thus time) that would be displayed on the page. The developers and I reached a happy medium that would allow for all user-goals to be met, as well as satisy load times.

Due to time, the amount of filters available on the first release was trimmed. We narrowed the filters down to a minimum viable product for each of the pages.

In the finals below, click throughs to the next page happen when clicking on the triple bar and are dependent on the filters selected.

Database Creation

information architecture, content strategy

Designed method to organize content to be used for data visualization.

Creating a Database

Role

information architect, content strategist, content manager

Responsibilities

Create schema for organization of all content, communicate implementation strategy to team, implement schema to organize all content, lead review process of content

Problem

Our company had tens of thousands of pieces of content that were not connected. In anticipation of state-specific variations of Common Core State Standards, we wanted to create a database consisting of all content in a way that was flexible enough to quickly align content to both high school state math standards and high school math textbooks so that we could produce products faster.

Requirements

  • Use existing content of 4 digital textbooks, 10,000 problems, and 1000 videos
  • Ability to mix and match content based on state standards and books
  • Must be able to incorporate new content

Solution

Aligned problems to learning objectives and then aligned learning objectives to standards and book lessons.

Team

project manager, product manager, 3+ content contractors, 3 developers

Timeline

1 year

Process

What content needs to be classified?

  • Created content analysis of all four digital textbooks
  • Determined which topics were taught in each section
  • Determined if similar topics were taught in other books
  • Took inventory of types of content: whiteboards (akin to short videos), problems, text, videos

How is the content connected?

  • Used content maps to demonstrate how content was connected across all books.
  • Determined that “Algebra 1” problems were not just in Algebra 1 and could appear in Pre-Algebra or Algebra 2.
  • Reiterated that current student math knowledge is dependent upon previous math knowledge.
  • Iterated on classifying the content.

Iteration 1

Proposed solution: Five layers of “tags” added to each piece of content.

Pros: Tags are the direction we should move in when classifying.

Cons: Applying five different tags to all content is unsustainable. It would be too much work to add 5 tags to 10,000 problems and review all of the tags!

Iteration 2

Proposed solution: One skill tag added to each problem.

Pros: One tag should be applied per problem.

Cons: Again, too much work to individually tag and review the tags of 10,000 problems.

Iteration 3

Our content was organized in such a way that short videos were connected to multiple problems. The last iteration involved tagging each video with one skill, which would then be automatically applied to the problems.

Tags, and their connected problems, could be grouped based on desired content needed for a standard or book. Tags could be in multiple products at once.

Successes and Learnings

This was my first major project within the tech industry. It was a rude awakening. Luckily, I had a team that trusted me and gave me constructive feedback along the way. I learned what was feasible within our system, and what was not feasible. I learned how to think about scalability and longterm maintenance.

Following the creation of the database, three additional digital texts came out within the course of several months using the database of tagged problems. The tag system has expanded to online reporting (data visualization) for teachers to determine areas where students are struggling and excelling.
